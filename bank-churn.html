The problem

A 20% churn rate hides very different stories

A bank's customer base rarely churns uniformly. Treating all 10,000 customers as one homogeneous group — with an overall churn rate of 20.4% — hides pockets of customers who are far more likely to leave, and others who are comfortably loyal.

The goal was twofold: first, segment customers into behaviorally distinct groups to see where retention risk actually concentrates. Second, build a model to predict individual churn risk — while making sure that model reflects genuine behavioral signal, not information that only exists because a customer already decided to leave.

Approach

From raw data to a leakage-checked churn model

01
Data Cleaning & EDA
Verified no missing values across 10,000 records; confirmed baseline churn rate of 20.4%.
02
Feature Engineering for Clustering
Prepared and scaled behavioral/demographic features for unsupervised segmentation.
03
Optimal Cluster Selection
Used the Elbow method (inertia) together with Silhouette score to validate the right number of segments — landed on k = 2.
04
Cluster Profiling
Profiled each segment by geography, balance, product count, and churn rate to find where risk concentrates.
05
Leakage Audit
Checked correlation of every feature against the churn target before modeling — caught one feature at 0.9957 correlation and excluded it.
06
Churn Modeling — Logistic Regression
Trained on the leakage-free feature set with standardized inputs and encoded categoricals (Geography, Gender, Card Type).
07
Probability Calibration
Applied CalibratedClassifierCV and validated with a reliability diagram comparing predicted vs. observed frequency before/after calibration.

Segmentation insight

The highest-balance segment churns the most

One cluster — almost entirely (99%) customers based in Germany, with an average balance roughly double that of the other segment (~120K vs. ~62K) — churned at 33.0%, more than double the 16.1% churn rate seen in the rest of the customer base.

This is the kind of pattern an aggregate 20.4% churn figure completely hides: the customers with the most money on deposit are also, in this dataset, the most likely to leave — exactly the segment where retention effort would have the highest financial payoff.

Challenges & solutions

What made this hard

Challenge

A feature ("Complain") showed 0.9957 correlation with the churn target — a number too good to be true.

Solution

Cross-tabulated the feature against the target directly (2,034 of 2,044 complainers churned) — confirmed it was recorded at/after the churn decision, not a real predictive signal. Excluded it from training.

Challenge

No ground-truth labels exist for "correct" customer segments — an arbitrary choice of k risks either oversimplifying or overfitting the segmentation.

Solution

Cross-checked the Elbow method against Silhouette score rather than relying on either alone, before settling on k = 2.

Challenge

A high AUC doesn't guarantee the predicted probabilities themselves are trustworthy for business use (e.g. prioritizing retention spend).

Solution

Applied CalibratedClassifierCV and compared reliability diagrams pre/post calibration — confirming ranking (AUC 0.7799 → 0.7792) stayed stable while probability reliability was checked directly rather than assumed.

Key learnings

What this project taught me

A feature with near-perfect correlation to the target is a red flag, not a gift — it usually means the feature is a consequence of the outcome (e.g. a complaint logged around cancellation), not a cause of it.

Segmentation can surface concentrated risk that an aggregate churn rate hides entirely — the highest-balance cluster here churned at more than 2x the rate of the rest.

AUC and calibration measure different things — a model can rank customers correctly while still needing validation on whether its predicted probabilities are usable for decisions like pricing or targeting.

Validating cluster count with two independent methods (Elbow + Silhouette) is a small step that meaningfully increases confidence in an otherwise unsupervised choice.
